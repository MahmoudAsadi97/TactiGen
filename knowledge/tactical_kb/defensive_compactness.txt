Compactness describes how small an area a team occupies relative to the full pitch when defending, and it is one of the most reliable indicators of defensive organisation. A compact team denies the opponent space to play between and behind its lines, forcing play into less dangerous wide or backward areas. Compactness has two dimensions: horizontal, the lateral distance across the defensive block, and vertical, the distance between the defensive, midfield, and attacking lines.

Vertical compactness is typically measured as the distance between the deepest defender and the most advanced presser, and is often kept around 25 to 35 metres in an organised mid-block. Horizontal compactness can be quantified by the distance between the widest players on the same line, or more holistically by the area of the convex hull enclosing all outfield players. A smaller hull area indicates a more compact shape.

Compactness is central to defending against overloads. By keeping lines close together, a team reduces the passing lanes available between units and makes it difficult for opponents to find a free man in central areas. When the block is compact, defenders can shift as a unit toward the ball, maintaining cover and balance so that no single channel becomes a 2v1.

There is, however, a trade-off between compactness and pressing intensity, and between compactness and total pitch coverage. To be compact, a team must concede space somewhere — usually the areas far from the ball and the space behind a high line or in front of a low block. A team pressing high must remain compact vertically to keep the striker and back line connected; if the lines stretch, gaps appear for opponents to receive between the lines.

In a low block, compactness is prioritised over pressing: the team drops deep, narrows toward its own goal, and protects the central zone and penalty area, accepting that the opponent will have the ball and width in front of them. In a mid block, the team balances compactness with selective pressing, holding shape around the halfway line and springing pressure on specific triggers.

Coaches train compactness through shadow play, line-coordination drills, and pressing references, teaching players to move in relation to the ball, the opponent, and their teammates simultaneously. The hardest skill is maintaining compactness during transitions and lateral ball movement, when the temptation to ball-watch or step out individually can fracture the block and expose the very spaces compactness is meant to protect.
