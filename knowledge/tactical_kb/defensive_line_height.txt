Defensive line height refers to how far up the pitch a team positions its back line, measured as the distance of the defensive line from its own goal. It is one of the most consequential decisions a defending team makes, because it governs the balance between the space conceded in front of the defence and the space conceded behind it.

A high defensive line is pushed well up the pitch, often near or beyond the halfway line. Its advantages are significant: it compresses the space between the lines, keeping the team compact and connected so opponents cannot receive comfortably between midfield and defence. A high line enables the offside trap, allowing defenders to step up in unison to catch attackers offside, and it supports a high press by keeping the back line close to the pressing midfielders and forwards. By squeezing the playing area, a high line forces the opponent to play in a congested space.

The risks of a high line are equally clear. The principal danger is the space left behind the defenders, which fast attackers can attack with runs in behind and which opponents can target with through balls and long diagonals. A high line demands defenders with good recovery pace, sharp offside coordination, and an alert goalkeeper willing to act as a sweeper. Against a quick centre-forward or a team that plays direct, a high line can be repeatedly exposed.

A low defensive line sits deep, close to the team's own penalty area. Its advantages are defensive security and the denial of space in behind: with little room between the back line and the goalkeeper, through balls lose their effectiveness and attackers must break down a packed defence in front of goal. The cost is that a low line invites pressure, concedes territory and possession, and leaves long distances to cover when transitioning to attack.

Line height should not be static; it must adapt to the phase of play and the opponent's offensive profile. Against a side with rapid forwards who thrive on space in behind, a team may drop its line to remove that threat. Against a possession-based opponent that builds patiently, a higher line and press may be used to deny time on the ball. Within a match, line height is adjusted continually — pushing up to compress play when the opponent is under pressure, dropping to protect the goal when defending a lead or facing a counter-attack. Coordinating line height with the press and midfield line is essential, since a back line that pushes up while the midfield drops opens the very space compactness is meant to deny.
