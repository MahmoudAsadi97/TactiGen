Team width refers to how much of the pitch's lateral space a team occupies, and it is a fundamental lever for manipulating the opponent's defensive shape. Width matters differently in and out of possession, and understanding the distinction is essential to using it effectively.

In possession, width is used to stretch the opponent's block horizontally. By stationing players on both touchlines, typically wingers or attacking full-backs, the attacking team forces the defending side to widen its back line and midfield to provide coverage. As the block widens, the distances between defenders increase, opening gaps in the half-spaces and central channels that attackers can exploit with passes between the lines, third-man runs, or dribbles into the seams. Width, in this sense, is a tool for creating central space rather than an end in itself.

Out of possession, width takes on a different meaning. Pressing width describes how far apart the defending team's wide players position themselves when applying pressure. A narrow defensive width keeps the team compact and protects central areas but concedes space wide; a wider pressing shape covers the flanks but risks stretching the block and opening central lanes. Teams adjust their out-of-possession width according to where they want to force the opponent, usually channelling play into wide areas where the touchline aids the press.

Wide midfielders and full-backs are the principal agents of width. In possession they provide and maintain width so the team does not become narrow and predictable; out of possession they must judge when to hold width and when to tuck in to preserve compactness. The coordination between winger and full-back on each side — who provides width and who provides depth or underlaps — is a central coaching question.

There is an optimal range rather than a single ideal: too little width allows the opponent to remain compact and crowd the centre, while too much width isolates players, lengthens passing distances, and can disconnect the attack from supporting runners. Many teams aim to occupy the full width in build-up and then narrow into the final third to attack the box with numbers.

Width is also directly connected to overload creation. By establishing maximum width on one side, a team can draw the opponent's block across, then quickly switch play to a now-isolated player on the opposite flank, converting width into a weak-side overload. Equally, holding width on the far side preserves the option of the switch when a near-side overload draws defenders toward the ball. Width and overload are therefore two sides of the same spatial manipulation.
