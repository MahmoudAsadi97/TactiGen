Overload and underload describe situations of numerical superiority and inferiority in a given zone of the pitch. An overload exists where the team in focus has more players than the opponent in a particular area; an underload is the reverse. Crucially, the two are connected: because each team has a fixed number of players, deliberately creating an overload in one zone necessarily accepts an underload in another.

Coaches exploit this trade-off intentionally. A common principle is to overload one side of the pitch to draw the opponent's defensive block toward the ball, then exploit the resulting underload — and the space it leaves — on the opposite side. The attacking team concentrates players and combinations on the strong side not necessarily to score from there, but to provoke the defensive shift that frees the weak side.

Central to overload thinking is the concept of the free man, a player who is unmarked because the opponent cannot cover every attacker in the zone. The free man is the outlet the overload is designed to produce: the player who can receive, turn, and progress or deliver without immediate pressure. Good build-up structures are organised to guarantee a free man, often by positioning one more player than the opponent presses with in the first line.

Underloads on the weak side are the setup for switches of play. When the opponent shifts across to deal with a strong-side overload, the far-side winger or full-back is frequently left in a 1v1 or entirely free. A quick switch — a long diagonal or a rapid sequence of passes across the block — relocates the ball to that underloaded area before the defence can recover its shape, turning a defensive numerical inferiority into an attacking advantage.

Defensively, teams must organise to handle overloads without conceding the spaces they protect. The principal tools are diagonal cover, with defenders positioning at angles so one can pressure while another covers; collective shifting toward the ball as a connected unit; and dropping to deny space in behind when outnumbered. A defender facing a 2v1 is taught to delay rather than dive in, buying time for recovering teammates to restore numerical balance.

The art of using overloads and underloads lies in timing and recognition. Attackers must read when the overload has drawn enough defenders to make the switch worthwhile, while defenders must judge when to shift, when to hold, and when to accept an overload in a low-value area to protect the centre. Numerical relationships, constantly shifting as play moves, underpin much of modern positional play and decision-making.
