A high press is a defensive strategy in which a team applies pressure to the opponent high up the pitch, close to the opponent's goal, with the aim of forcing turnovers in dangerous areas or disrupting the build-up before it develops. Rather than retreating into shape, the pressing team advances its lines and attempts to suffocate the opponent's first phase of possession.

Pressing is governed by triggers — specific cues that signal the moment to engage. Common triggers include the goalkeeper or a centre-back taking a heavy or backward touch, a pass played backward under pressure, a player receiving with his back to play, or a long, hopeful ball to an isolated striker who can be surrounded on the second ball. Recognising and reacting to triggers collectively is what separates an organised press from individual chasing.

A key tactical device is the pressing trap, in which the defending team deliberately leaves an option open — often a pass to a full-back near the touchline — and then collapses on that player once the ball is travelling. The touchline acts as an extra defender, reducing the trapped player's options and increasing the chance of a turnover in a wide, advanced area.

Defensive line height is tightly linked to press effectiveness. To press high, the back line must also push up to keep the team compact; otherwise large gaps open between midfield and defence for opponents to exploit between the lines. A high press therefore necessarily accepts a high line and the space in behind that comes with it.

The principal risk of a failed press is transition vulnerability. If the opponent plays through or over the first wave of pressure, the pressing team is left exposed with numbers committed forward and space behind a high line — ideal conditions for a fast counter-attack. Successful pressing teams therefore rehearse rest defence: the positioning of the players not directly involved in the press so they can delay and screen if possession is lost.

PPDA, or passes allowed per defensive action, is a widely used proxy for pressing intensity. It counts the number of opponent passes permitted in a defined area before the defending team makes a tackle, interception, foul, or challenge. A low PPDA indicates that the team allows few passes before acting, signalling an aggressive, high-intensity press; a high PPDA indicates a more passive approach. Coaches monitor PPDA alongside line height and compactness to judge whether the press is functioning as intended, and adjust the triggers and shape when opponents begin to play through it.
